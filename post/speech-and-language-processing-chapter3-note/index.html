N-gram Language Models

2022-10-31 11 min read # NLP # Reading Note # Speech and Language Processing

Intuition and assumption of N-gram; Maximum likelihood extimation(MLE); Smoothing tricks; Perplexity, entropy and the evaluation of language models;

Speech and Language Processing - Chapter 3 Note

Language models assign probabilities to seqs of words, and n-gram models are one of the most foundational kinds of LMs.

1. Intuition behind N-gram:

Calculating the probability of a word based on the entire history, i.e. P(wn∣w1:n−1)P(w_n|w_{1:n-1})P(wn​∣w1:n−1​), is (1) too complicated, although it's possible to apply chain rules for the joint probability P(w1:n)=P(w1)P(w2∣w1)...P(wn∣w1:n−1)P(w_{1:n})=P(w_1)P(w_2|w_1)...P(w_n|w_{1:n-1})P(w1:n​)=P(w1​)P(w2​∣w1​)...P(wn​∣w1:n−1​) (2) impossible to model human language, since its context could be creative and any particular context might have never occurred before. Thus, here comes the important Markov approximation:

P(wn∣w1:n−1)≈P(wn∣wn−1)P(w_n|w_{1:n-1}) \approx P(w_n|w_{n-1}) P(wn​∣w1:n−1​)≈P(wn​∣wn−1​)

2. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Use MLE to estimate for the parameters for an n-gram model. Still, there are different approaches to it, like relative frequency. It's both intuitive and simple.

Note: Why add start-symbol <s> and end-symbol </s> in the alphabet?
To get a proper probabilistic distribution. We want the probabilities for all sentences of differentl lengths summing to 1, but without these special tokens, N-gram model would define an infinite set of probability distributions with one distribution per sentence length (i.e. the probabilities of all sentences for a given sentence length sum up to 1)

But using relative frequency has its problems, the most prominent one is the problem of zero probability, caused by unknown words or unseen events. This problem must be handled, especially when calculating PPL. Unknown words in the test set can be marked as <UNK>, and unseen events can be dealt with Smoothing

3. Evaluating LMs

extrinsic evaluation <-> intrinsic evaluation

  1. Perplexity(PPL)
    PPL is a function of both the text and the language model, the geometric mean of the inverse test set probability computed by the model. The PPL of two LMs is only comparable if they use identical vocabularies.
    For a test set WWW, general form:

perplexity(W)=1P(w1w2w3...wN)N=∏i=1N1P(wi∣w1w2...wi−1)N\mathrm{perplexity}(W)=\sqrt[N]{\frac{1}{P(w_1w_2w_3...w_N)}}\\ =\sqrt[N]{\prod^N_{i=1}\frac{1}{P(w_i|w_1w_2...w_{i-1})}} perplexity(W)=NP(w1​w2​w3​...wN​)1​​=Ni=1∏N​P(wi​∣w1​w2​...wi−1​)1​​

perplexity(W)=∏i=1N1P(wi∣wi−1)N\mathrm{perplexity}(W)=\sqrt[N]{\prod_{i=1}^N\frac{1}{P(w_i|w_{i-1})}} perplexity(W)=Ni=1∏N​P(wi​∣wi−1​)1​​

  2. Perplexity’s Relation to Entropy

Entropy:

H(X)=−∑x∈Xp(x)logp(x) H(X)=-\sum_{x\in \mathcal X}p(x)logp(x) H(X)=−x∈X∑​p(x)logp(x)

Entropy of a language (modelled as a stochatic process):

1nH(L)=−1n∑w1:n∈Lp(w1:n)logp(w1:n)\frac{1}{n}H(L)=-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{w_{1:n}\in L}p(w_{1:n})logp(w_{1:n}) n1​H(L)=−n1​w1:n​∈L∑​p(w1:n​)logp(w1:n​)

The Shannon-McMillan-Breiman theorem states that if the language is both stationary and ergotic, the entropy rate of a language is defined as:

H(L)=lim⁡n→∞−1nlogp(w1w2...wn)H(L)=\lim_{n\to \infty}-\frac{1}{n}logp(w_1w_2...w_n) H(L)=n→∞lim​−n1​logp(w1​w2​...wn​)

Cross Entropy: We don’t know the actual probability distribution ppp that generates data, and model mmm is an approximation of ppp.

H(p,m)=lim⁡n→∞−1n∑W∈Lp(w1,...,wn)logm(w1,...,wn)H(p,m)=\lim_{n\to\infty}-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{W\in L}p(w_1,...,w_n)logm(w_1,...,w_n) H(p,m)=n→∞lim​−n1​W∈L∑​p(w1​,...,wn​)logm(w1​,...,wn​)

Again, following the Shannon-Mcmillan-Breiman theorem:

H(p,m)=lim⁡n→∞logm(w1,...,wn)H(p,m)=\lim_{n\to\infty}logm(w_1,...,w_n) H(p,m)=n→∞lim​logm(w1​,...,wn​)

And it’s proven that CE H(p,m)H(p,m)H(p,m) is the upper bound of entropy H(m)H(m)H(m):

H(m)≤H(p,m)H(m)\le H(p,m) H(m)≤H(p,m)

Their gap can be viewed as the measure of how model mmm approximates distribution ppp. The lower CE, the more accurate the model.

The approximation to CE of a model M=P(wi∣wi−N+1:i−1)M=P(w_i|w_{i-N+1:i-1})M=P(wi​∣wi−N+1:i−1​) on a sequence of words WWW is:

H(W)=−1NlogP(w1w2...wN)H(W)=-\frac{1}{N}logP(w_1w_2...w_N) H(W)=−N1​logP(w1​w2​...wN​)

Therefore, we have:

Perplexity(W)=2H(W)\mathrm{Perplexity}(W)=2^{H(W)} Perplexity(W)=2H(W)

To sum up, cross entropy can measure how accurate the language model is. Sampling a sequence of words with an LM on a test set and calculating its probability get us an approximation of the cross entropy, which is also the transformed perplexity. That explains why PPL can be used to evaluate LMs.
